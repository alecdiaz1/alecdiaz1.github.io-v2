WebReg Redesign

Redesigning WebReg to Better Serve Students

Summary

As a UCI student who has to add/drop classes every quarter, UCI’s online enrollment system WebReg has worried me more than once about whether or not I have actually succeeded in adding and dropping classes. Additionally, I remember as a new UCI student, I had trouble understanding what certain buttons and fields were for, and what menus I needed to use to accomplish my tasks. I performed user research and redesigned WebReg to better fit user’s needs and expectations.

Role

UX/UI Designer

Tools

Figma

ProtoPie

Photoshop

Timeline

October 8, 2018 -
October 15, 2018

The Problem

WebReg serves a specific purpose to UCI students: to check their enrollment window, check their fee status, and manage/view their waitlist and enrollment in classes. In its current form, it can be confusing to accomplish basic tasks, especially for new students. Some of the issues include:

  • Cumbersome process to enroll and waitlist classes
  • Unused features
  • Confusing vocabulary

Research

Since WebReg is exclusive to UCI, I interviewed four UCI students of three different class levels to identify some of their pain points and one student from another university as an additional outside perspective. This way, I could identify pain points from users of differing levels of familiarity with WebReg.


I asked a series of seven open-ended questions and asked the interviewees to rank WebReg’s current features based on their importance. Through this, I was able to learn about some of the issues the students commonly face with WebReg in addition to findings that contradicted my original assumptions.



Key Quotes

  • “We can check fee status? I’ve literally never clicked on it.”
  • “You have to go back to the main menu so many times.
  • “The part for dropping/adding/changing classes can get a little confusing, especially since you have to copy and paste the class number.”
  • “It’s pretty straightforward but slow.”
  • “More of the windows being available all on one screen would be helpful.”
  • “It's extremely cumbersome to find and plan courses.”

Synthesizing Data and Forming Insights

During the interview, I also asked the interviewees to rank WebReg’s current features on importance, and if they knew what certain features did.

  • All of the interviewees did not know what the “change” feature does
  • 60% of the interviewees did not know what “the “reset” button does
  • All of the interviewees listed add/drop class from enrollment and waitlist as their #1 task when on WebReg

Most of the users shared similar opinions in prioritizing tasks and similar emotional responses to navigating WebReg and performing some actions. Some of the feelings that users associate with WebReg included slow, simple, inefficient, normal, straightforward, and frustration.


Combining the qualitative data and quantitative data from user interviews led to a few key insights:

  • Students are confused by the number of fields and buttons
  • Students are slowed down by the number of pages needed to do each task
  • Students do not use some of the current features
  • Some students find the process straightforward but cumbersome


Though most of the users found WebReg inefficient and slow, to my surprise, one user actually said that it was simple and straightforward. While some users had different viewpoints on the usability of WebReg, I found that all of the users did agree that some of the features were redundant and confusing.


When brainstorming, I kept these responses in mind. I explored two different designs, trying to reduce redundancy and increase the efficiency of all the features such as adding and dropping classes, while still keeping the process straightforward.

Ideation





Overall, my goal was to increase efficiency and reduce redundancy in the interface. On the home page, the student is able to see their fee status, enrollment window, and any recent notifications for their classes. In doing this, I was able to remove two buttons and make notifications clearer. Previously, notifications were very vague and said only "one or more of your classes has changed..."


For adding and dropping classes, I simplified the process by introducing a search bar where a student can just search for a class and add it instantly by clicking. This also removes the need for the many of the buttons and options students previously said they never used. In addition, the green box gives the student a positive or negative response depending on if they were able to add the class.


To drop a class or more than one class, a student only has to check off the boxes and click the trash button, then confirm the deletion. The red box gives the student a notification that they have successfully dropped the class.

Conclusion

Since my goal was to reduce redundancy and increase efficiency, I designed the interface to be simple but functional. I believe my redesign is very effective because it greatly streamlines the entire process of adding and dropping classes. Additionally, it gives the student clearer indication on the status of the system after they perform an action. Due to time constraints, I did not implement a weekly calendar feature that I had planned to do.

Takeaways

  • How to conduct better user research
  • How to use Figma and ProtoPie
  • Finding data that opposes my initial assumptions

Conducting real user research for the first time was a fun and rewarding experience. In the future, I would try to get more users from diverse backgrounds for interviews. In addition, I would like to try other user research methods such as focus groups. In conclusion, this exercise was a fun introduction into user research and taught me more about what I need to incorporate into my design process.
